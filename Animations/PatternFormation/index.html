Pattern formation

Well... I came across this article the other day, and found it fascinating. In it, a simple numerical method based on chem-trails and chemoattraction of simple organisms is shown to reproduce complex patterns. Without going into much detail, I decided to play with the idea here. After playing a bit with it, it turned out to give very pretty results, so I decided to make it into a automatic-art thing.

My approach was the following: I crate an object, called a walker (create with mouseclick), whose main properties is a position, a direction, and a color $\mathbf{c}_0$ (or rule). Each step, a walker walks toward its direction at a preset, but somewhat fluctuating, length. After moving, it interacts with a background color mesh, either by smearing nearby pixels (the SMEAR option above) or by pulling the pixel underneath it towards its color $\mathbf{c}_0$ The walker has a list of locations in front of it from which it may sample the pixel color. Each walker carries a cost-function $f=f(\mathbf{c},\mathbf{c}_0)$ whose value depends on the sensed color $\mathbf{c}$ and its own color $\mathbf{c}_0$. Armed with the cost function, the walker is asked to change its direction towards the sensed location with minimal cost. One natural choice of cost-function is $f_{a+}=|\mathbf{c}-\mathbf{c}_0|^2$, which measures the color-space distance to its own color $\mathbf{c}_0$. This cost function is the one labelled autophilic. The opposite is a walker that walks towards the color that is maximally different from its own color, i.e. $f = f_{a+}$ (autophobic).
