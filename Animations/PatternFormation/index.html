Pattern formation

Well... I came across this article the other day, and found it fascinating. In it, a simple numerical method based on chem-trails and chemoattraction of simple organisms is shown to reproduce complex patterns. Without going into much detail, I decided to play with the idea here. After playing a bit with it, it turned out to give very pretty results, so I decided to make it into a automatic-art thing.

My approach was the following: I crate an object, called a walker (create with mouseclick), whose main properties is a position, a direction, and a color $\mathbf{c}_0$ (or rule). The walker has a list of locations in front of it from which it may sample the pixel color. Each walker carries a cost-function $f=f(\mathbf{c},\mathbf{c}_0)$ whose value depends on the sensed color $\mathbf{c}$ and its own color $\mathbf{c}_0$. Armed with the cost function, the walker is asked to change its direction towards the sensed location with minimal cost. One natural choice of cost-function is $f_{a+}=|\mathbf{c}-\mathbf{c}_0|^2$, which measures the color-space distance to its own color $\mathbf{c}_0$. This cost function is the one labelled philic. The opposite is a walker that walks towards the color that is maximally different from its own color, i.e. $f = f_{a+}$ (phobic). There is also an option to change the definition of $\mathbf{c}_0$ (away from its own color self) to to darkness (black), brightness (white) or to any other preset color (input). The locations in front is defined by an angular span (sensewidth) and a length (senselength).

Once the direction is updated based on the sensed pixel-response, the walker steps toward its direction at a predefined, but somewhat fluctuating, length. After moving, it interacts with a background color mesh, either by smearing nearby pixels (the SMEAR option above) or by pulling the pixel underneath it towards its color $\mathbf{c}_0$.

I probably forgot to mention a lot, but I have confidence you will figure things out. Have fun!
